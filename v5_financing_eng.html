Islamic Finance Fundamentals

What is Islamic Finance?

Islamic finance operates under Shariah (Islamic law) principles that prohibit interest (riba), excessive uncertainty (gharar), and investment in prohibited sectors. Instead of charging interest, Islamic banks structure transactions as trade, leasing, or profit-sharing arrangements.

Key Structures Used in PROJECT K:

  • Ijara (Islamic Leasing): Bank purchases assets, leases them back to the client, and transfers ownership at completion
  • Sukuk (Islamic Bonds): Asset-backed securities that provide returns through profit-sharing rather than interest
  • Musharaka (Partnership): Bank and client co-invest in a venture and share profits/losses proportionally

UAE Islamic Finance Leadership: The UAE hosts 70% of global Sukuk issuances, with Dubai Islamic Bank ($97B assets) and ADIB leading the market[6].

Islamic Finance Market Access

Why Islamic Finance Works Better for Permanent Financing

Revenue-Based Structures: Operating assets generate cash flows that align perfectly with Islamic profit-sharing principles, while construction assets create ownership complications under Shariah compliance frameworks[18].

Market Access: Nasdaq Dubai listing provides access to $3+ trillion in Islamic assets under management globally[5], including UAE sovereign wealth funds and GCC institutional investors.

Cost Efficiency: Mature operating assets receive investment-grade pricing, with recent UAE Sukuk issued at 4.5-5.5% vs. 6.5-7.5% construction financing rates[19].

Structural Advantages of Sequential Financing:

  • Eliminates Islamic-Korean Legal Conflicts: UAE Islamic Finance Law requires bank asset ownership during construction[20], conflicting with Korean bank first-lien requirements under Korean Banking Act[21]
  • Optimizes Government Benefits: Sequential structure captures maximum KEXIM subsidies during construction, then accesses low-cost Islamic capital for permanent financing
  • Reduces Transaction Complexity: Clean handoff eliminates complex cross-border Islamic finance approvals during critical construction phase

Three-Phase Capital Deployment Framework

Strategic Rationale for Sequential Structure

Conflict Avoidance: Islamic Ijara structures require bank ownership of construction assets, creating irreconcilable conflicts with Korean bank first-lien security requirements. Sequential financing eliminates these structural impediments while capturing benefits from both systems.

Subsidy Optimization: Korean bridge financing during construction phases, followed by KEXIM Export Finance permanent financing, maximizes government subsidy capture while providing optimal cost of capital across the asset lifecycle.

Risk Management: Each phase validates market demand and operational performance before committing subsequent capital, enabling progressive improvement in financing terms and reducing execution risk.
